AngelList is dead, long live AngelList!

I’m going to use the blog this week to say, “I told you so.”

Since Tuesday, the most talked about story in our office has been AngelList. If you haven’t already heard, it was Tuesday when AngelList announced that new venture firm CSC Upshot said they’re committing $400 million to fund investments on AngelList. CSC will be run by one of China’s largest private equity firms, China Science & Merchants Investment, which has $12 billion under management.

There are so many interesting facets to this story. Sarah Lacy at Pando Media wrote an insightful article, but other than Sarah’s piece, predictably, the mainstream media has covered not a single aspect of what makes this deal noteworthy.

Since this is such a juicy topic, for now, I’m only focusing on the I-told-you-so part of the story and I’ll get back to what makes a CSC/Chinese deal so interesting in a later blog post.

For anyone who’s listened to me talk about equities deal marketing on the Internet over the past couple of years, there’s a prediction I’ve made repeatedly: The business of raising capital online will follow a similar trajectory as P2P lending.

As a quick refresher, the earliest consumer lending websites like Prosper and Lending Club were built on the premise of individuals loaning money to individuals – hence the “P2P” (peer-to-peer). Over the span of only a few recent years, institutional investors began deploying capital into the P2P lending space, using all the available data online to make rapid – and increasingly larger – investment decisions. Fast forward to the modern day lending environment, so much institutional capital has been pumped into the online lending market that nobody even calls it P2P anymore. Instead, it’s widely called “marketplace lending.”

That’s because lending websites have replaced the “peers” in their business model with institutional capital – or what we at Dealflow refer to as “real” capital. After all, who needs to deal with thousands of individuals when demand for your product exceeds the supply? A few weeks ago I wrote about how Prosper was paying over 10 times more for a borrower lead than an investor lead, which points not just to supply/demand problems but to heavy saturation of cash in that space.

The AngelList announcement does more than just hint at what’s coming in the online equities market, it makes my P2P prediction look like reality.

AngelList is a company that holds incredible sway in the startup ecosystem. Much of their clout is derived from its close ties to the founders running all those startups, specifically those founders who’ve had exits and made a bunch of money.

AngelList doesn’t have to brag about – or lie about, depending on your level of cynicism – tens of thousands of accredited investors on its website like many other deal platforms do. They state the actual number on their blog – 4,400.

I have a strong suspicion that the majority of these startup investors are startup founders themselves. This isn’t some wild guess. Spend 10 minutes on AngelList’s website and you’ll get a sense of the demographics.

If there’s a hiccup in the startup ecosystem that affects the appetite for AngelList’s risky assets, then investment from startup founders will be quick to dry up. Everyone knows this hiccup is coming.

To be sure, $400 million is a massive amount of cash to deploy into early-stage deals that average about $1 million per raise. In fact, in the Wall Street Journal coverage of the CSC announcement, AngelList’s CEO Naval Ravikant was quoted as saying, “We’re limited in how much capital we can absorb.” And he says that the money will be doled out slowly so as not to overwhelm the market.

How prominently founder-investors will feature in AngelList’s future agenda is pure speculation. AngelList may very well start to resemble OurCrowd in its investment model – that is, deploy capital from an institutional fund and round out raises with money from individuals. If they go this route, it might be a smart strategy. They’d keep individuals engaged and they could stick to their existing syndicates business model, which relies largely on individuals to find and vet new deals.

One thing that’s not speculation though is that the CSC deal effectively back-doors AngelList right into the asset management business. With a cash pot of $400 million, they don’t need fickle investor-founders slowing down an investment process. Where the syndicate model breaks down is that most users of AngelList soft-commit to invest behind a syndicate lead, get to check out that lead’s deal flow, and more often than not don’t follow through with making an investment. This makes it difficult to predict whether or not cash will actually come into a deal that’s being promoted. $400 million takes care of that problem.

I’m predicting that the CSC investment will be a proxy for institutional investors who will increasingly depend on Internet-based deal flow to deploy capital. They’ll be looking toward online deal inventory and where that inventory is strongest there’ll be an enormous flow of money. Over time, individual investors will be marginalized.

In the Wall Street Journal article AngelList's CEO said he believes this is just the beginning and that “$400 million is just the tip of the spear.” I couldn’t agree more.
