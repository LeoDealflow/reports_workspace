Why Venture Capital Fundraising will Never be the Same

Just a few years ago, companies doing an early-stage round with the assistance of a broker would’ve been laughed out of a venture capitalist’s office. The broker could’ve been a friend acting as deal-finder or a pro from an investment bank. In either case, if you had an intermediary you weren’t exactly greeted with the red carpet. If the deal was brokered, it had a certain taint.

Well, this has changed – big time.

The business of raising capital for venture-stage companies now has a huge online component. Forget the fact that Uber raised capital on AngelList. Pretty much every company seems to be raising at least some of their capital online. And whether companies are doing it with the assistance of VC funds, syndicate vehicles, or FINRA-registered websites, at the end of the day, they’re all brokers. Yep, regular intermediaries who earn broker economics if a company gets funded and/or becomes successful.

Even the online VCs putting their own cash into deals are brokering. You don’t believe me? Look closely at their investment terms to see how they get paid.

The origins of the taint come from the fact that nobody likes a shopped deal. And if the deal has a broker, it’s safe to assume the deal’s been shopped. What every investor wants is “proprietary dealflow.” It’s the Holy Grail in the deal business.

How does one get good, truly proprietary dealflow? It’s a question I think about often because even though I’m not a professional investor, I’ve spent my career serving investors with information. And so to understand their mindset is important to me. To be clear, in this context, “proprietary” means that a deal is brought to a fund directly from a company’s management, or from one of the fund’s own LPs, or is found by the fund through a proactive outsourcing effort – with no auction process, no shopping, no intermediary.

There’s no doubt that proprietary deals are special. From the investor’s point of view, they can get a deal closed without any bidding war over price or structure. Unfortunately for investors, proprietary dealflow is going the way of the dodo.

With certain exceptions (such as follow-on investments from existing shareholders), venture-stage companies aren’t just taking the proprietary deal route anymore. Instead, they’re moving their fundraising – or at least a part of it – online.

Maybe it’s that a younger generation is more open to experimenting with the Internet. Maybe it’s increasing investment demand from high-net worth individuals. Maybe it’s that broader distribution might mean better pricing. I’m not sure. But it’s happening.

I’ve noticed a serious uptick in the quality of companies being promoted in email. My inbox has been flooded with interesting deals. And as I’ve been going through these emails, I’ve found myself amazed at the quality of companies being pitched to me. I’m seeing companies with great traction, with products I actually use, and backed by brand-name funds.

For me, only part of the story is what I call the “retailization of private placements.” The larger story is that these are brokered deals. And that’s a big change in market dynamics which opens up all kinds of opportunities for those providing financial services.

Over the summer, I met with Byron Roth, CEO of investment bank Roth Capital. Byron was a client of my prior company DealFlow Media, which covered small cap equity deals, like PIPEs. I knew Roth as a firm that only focused on public companies so I wanted to talk with him when I saw private company fundraising announcements with Roth’s name listed as agent.

Byron said that they moved into banking private companies because he “finds good deals there.” Now maybe that was an intentionally terse and diplomatic response. But I suspect that like me, he’s looking beyond what worked in the past (like PIPEs) and looking forward with lots of help from leading indicators – all of which are online.
