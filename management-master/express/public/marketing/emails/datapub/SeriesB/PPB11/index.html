Regulation Red Tape and a not-so-crappy idea for Crowdfunding

I’ve been a startup guy my entire life. I started and sold a telecom software company in my 20’s. I started and sold a financial media company in my 30’s. And now that I’m in my 40’s, I’m doing it again with Dealflow.com. Even in my teens I had a tie dye t-shirt business I ran out of my mom’s basement. Oddly, my current partner Mike was my partner in the t-shirt company too. I guess things come full circle.

Anyway, with all these ventures under my belt I’ve learned a thing or two about what it takes to start a company and run it successfully. I’m not perfect but I like to think that I’ve been open to criticism and I’ve learned that you need to know a little bit about a lot of stuff if you’re going to make it in a startup.

Sure, everyone’s familiar with business axioms like “collect early and pay late” and “the customer is always right.” But for me, one of the most critical things I’ve learned is the importance of eliminating bureaucracy wherever possible – especially at the startup stage. So it’s with this as a backdrop that I read Regulation Crowdfunding this past week.

My main takeaway from Regulation Crowdfunding is that there’s a lot of red tape and a lot of legal and accounting rules to comply with if you’re an issuer, funding portal, or advisor working on a crowdfunded deal. So having good counsel to deal with all the bureaucracy should be high on your priority list.

Since there’s already plenty of information out there that summarizes the new rules, I thought I’d do a “Dealflow Mailbag” and address just a couple of the 50+ comments I received after last week’s blog. I cherry-picked the ones I thought I could add my two cents to without having to be admitted to the legal bar.

From David P: “My take on the new rules is that they are easy on issuers, which is totally essential if anyone is going to use them. The Q&A disclosure, not requiring tax returns, and the allowance to skip the audit the first time around are all great…Plus the thing where platforms can take an equity stake in the companies that list with them is super interesting and I don't even know what that's going to end up looking like.”

My 2 cents: The type of equity compensation that a platform takes would be limited to whatever securities were being offered to investors (see p. 165 of the Regulation). Since most of these companies will be issuing common stock that has some kind of cash equivalent value, that should temper any enthusiasm to take equity in lieu of cash fees because ordinary income tax would presumably apply based on the cash equivalent value. This is why bankers typically structure their non-cash compensation in the form of warrants. Who wants to pay income tax on equity which has only a possibility – but not a guarantee – of being turned back into cash? Kiran Lingam countered this in my LinkedIn post by pointing out that many platforms are likely to be operating at a loss as they ramp up and therefore, they’ll be able to offset that income. Fair point.

From Charlie O: “In the end, it appears that most of the responsibilities to comply with the rules falls on funding platforms and not issuers. That will add a lot of pressure to those companies but should give issuers and investors more comfort that things are being done right.”

My 2 cents: I’m fairly convinced that for crowdfunding to work in a meaningful way, investors are going to need comfort that issuers and funding platforms won’t abscond with their hard-earned dough. One way to create this level of comfort would be through a program that resembles SIPC and forces registered funding portals to participate by paying into the program according to a percentage of their net revenues. SIPC, or the Securities Investor Protection Corporation is a non-profit, non-government membership corporation funded by FINRA broker-dealers. All brokerage firms that sell stocks and bonds are required to be members of SIPC. Seems to me that a similar framework could be developed by the crowdfunding community to reimburse investors who were victims of fraud and have no recourse against a platform if that platform is insolvent. This might be necessary since the SEC did away with the proposed bond that the portals were going to have to post. I’d suggest calling this SIPC-like program the Crowdfunding Remuneration and Protection Fund, or “CRAP Fund” for short. (BTW, Mike gets credit for this idea.)

The agenda includes a who’s who of thought leaders in this area and is sure to be informative. I’ll see you there.
