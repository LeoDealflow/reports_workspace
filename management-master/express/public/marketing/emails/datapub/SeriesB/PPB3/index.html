Stop wondering why real estate "crowdfunding" and online lending are booming (Hint: It's the data)

The other day I got a phone call from a writer at the New York Times looking for data on the largest crowdfunding deals marketed online. After I got through 5 minutes of legal mumbo-jumbo on how technically there is no equity crowdfunding because of the delay in SEC rules (c'mon, you know the explanation I'm talking about), I asked him about the story he was writing.

He said he was covering a company claiming to be launching the largest equity crowdfunding deal ever. I was quick to ask what kind of real estate deal it was. Then I explained to him that it wasn't hard to figure out it's a real estate deal because for the most part, those are the "crowdfunding" deals having fairly widespread success online. Note: Here I’m using “crowdfunding” like he understood crowdfunding, with complete disregard for the legal mumbo-jumbo.

As we've been building Dealflow.com, we've taken a lot of cues from the world of online real estate databases. And a few months ago, I was lucky to meet Dennis DeAndre, who was the founder of LoopNet. Dennis started LoopNet at a time when the World Wide Web was in its infancy. And he pioneered commercial real estate listings online.

In my discussions with Dennis, it became clear why real estate databases and companies like LoopNet had huge growth. It had less to do with the number of market participants or other industry factors, and everything to do with the nature of data associated with real estate listings.

With real estate, there's a lot of structured and reliable data that accompanies deals. I call this “hard-data” because it lacks any ambiguity. Lease terms, square footage, property type, etc., are some of the many hard-data points that someone can look at and quickly determine if a deal is of interest.

Operating companies (as opposed to real estate projects) don't have nearly as many of these hard-data points. Most companies raising capital online are focused on technology ventures that require IP assessment, competitive analysis and subjective assumptions. The data isn’t as conducive to quick investment analysis.

Similarly, when you consider the boom in marketplace finance, you've got the same hard-data attributes associated with lending. FICO scores, past/present employment, credit history – these are all solid points of reference to help answer the question, "Is this an investment I'm interested in?"

I heard recently that Prosper.com is paying $25 for an investor lead and $350 for a borrower lead. If that’s accurate – and based on my source I believe it is – then it’s clear that demand for online credit products far exceeds the supply. Talk about a marketing problem!

The key to successful deal marketing – or marketing any products for that matter – comes down to the effective use of data. To be sure, real estate portals and online lenders are a positive leading indicator for where the online deal movement is heading.

We might have to guess as to what pockets of online deal activity will pique institutional interest like these sectors did. But there’s no need to guess that access to clear and reliable data will be the driving force behind it.
