When I attended college at George Washington University in the early 1990’s I had long curly hair, practiced guitar religiously, and spent a lot of time playing in a mediocre rock band. When our band was failing to deliver an audience for the bar we gigged at, we tried everything to put the word out and generate interest.

We hung flyers all over campus. We changed the color of the flyers. We tried psychedelic graphics. And when the flyers were pulled down by campus security, we put them right back up.

We were relentless but still, nothing worked. Week after week our fan base dwindled until we found ourselves playing to our roommates and the bar regulars.

Then one day I had the bright idea to change the name of our band to “Free Beer.” My thinking was that there’s nothing more appealing than free beer to college students. And to be fair, “Free Beer tonight at 10 PM” written on band flyers would actually be true if our name was Free Beer.

Well it shouldn’t come as much of a surprise that we packed the bar at our first gig with the new name. It also shouldn’t come as a surprise that it was the last gig we ever played.

In retrospect, this was an incredibly flawed marketing plan which resulted in a bunch of disgruntled college kids demanding their $5 cover back.

I was reminded of the perils of false – or can I say, faulty – advertising during this week’s regular marketing meeting at Dealflow. One of our biz dev people was giving me feedback she received from clients of a popular deal website. The feedback included several companies who were advised by the website to embellish their financial performance in order to generate more investor interest. Feedback coming from investors using that website was also predictably less than stellar.

After getting this bit of feedback, I started paying closer attention to the wordsmithing in emails from websites pitching startup investments. What I found – while maybe not complete fabrication – does call into question the integrity of the information (and the information provider too). Specifically, I found financial estimates that weren’t couched as proforma but were stated more like facts with a lot of murkiness on prior year, current year, and future year revenues/profits. Again, not fabrication, but not really straightforward either.

Then I started wondering what’s up with some of the details on deal profiles that convey how much “investment” went into a company during the past week. Is that really investment? Or is that a non-binding commitment to make an investment?

Seems to me there’s a pretty big difference. And for a research group like ours, it drives us nuts. Do we report an investment? Or do we report a loose promise to make an investment?

The same standards of straightforwardness should hold true not just for deal-specific marketing, but for marketing online deal platforms themselves. If you’re a private placement banker working with companies to issue securities in reliance on 80 year old securities rules, please don’t advertise yourselves as helping companies raise money using “crowdfunding.” It’s not semantics. It’s an important distinction because you aren’t conducting non-accredited crowdfunding.

To be sure, there’s lots of room for improvement in our nascent area of the capital markets. But in general, I find that people who preach integrity are those who have very little of it so I tend to tread lightly when being critical. Really, I’m just interested in accurate information.

With this in mind, please, the next time you consider marketing “free beer” in my direction, make sure you actually have some for me when I show up.
